The opportunities within chemical engineering are so broad, your role can be anything from transform raw materials into products via chemical processes, such as using oil to create plastics, fuel to power the world. Chemical engineers work in a variety of industries, including safety and risk, food and drink, textiles and pharmaceuticals, and the skills acquired from studying this course can be used to develope new products, from trial through to commercialisation, and making improvements to product lines and systems.Your skills could take you even beyond the world of engineering and can lead you into management, finance and business if you please. Ships, submarines and military vessels are just a selection of the vehicles you'll build, maintain and repair as a marine engineer (also known as a naval architect). You may also work on their water distillation and fuel systems, propulsion mechanics and ventilation systems, and test their engines for safe and efficient operation. Some aspects of the job, such as on-ship inspections, require working in high-pressure, physically demanding environments. The design and research elements of your work, however, will take place in an office or laboratory. A relevant degree, accredited by a body such as the Royal Institution of Naval Architects (RINA), will provide you with the technical and nautical knowledge, including use of complex computer systems and technical drawings, needed for this indust. The role of an electrical engineer is to keep the world connected, through all sorts of methods, through management, maintenance and development of broadcasting, transport and power channels.This can be achieved by designing, developing and testing equipment, such as resistors and transistors, to be applied to a range of areas, from mobile phone technology to medical equipment.
